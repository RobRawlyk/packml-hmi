PackML Implementation Guide

Part 6: PackML HMIs - User Interfaces, Stack Lights, and Push-Button Controls

Summary

[0001] This document – focusing on the design of Human-Machine Interfaces – constitues Part 6 in the series of PackML Implementation Guidelines. Targeting the engineers responsible for machine design, the contents of this part will establish standards and best practices in the areas of interaction and interface design. Coverage offers concrete examples explaining the guidelines, as well as reference material for those seeking more in depth explanations of the associated concepts.

2. Executive Summary

2.1 Purpose

[0002] It is generaly accepted that production lines with widely varying HMI designs will suffer from reduced operator efficiency. It follows that this lack of efficiency will lead to longer downtime events, lower throughput, and reduced job satisfaction. To help counter these issues, the following guidance will establish a set of Human-Machine Interface implementation standards that, when employed by packaging machinery designers, will establish the consistency necessary to accommodate the specific needs of the packaging-line operator.

[0003] In addition to the standards established in this document, the guidance will communicate a collection of more generic interface design best practices. We argue that the general level of usability within machine HMIs can always be improved. Accordingly, this document will provide guidance in the areas of usability and graphic design, which should benefit design engineers seeking to expand their background in those disciplines.

[0004] To accompany the recommendations offered throughout, this guideline presents a collection of reference material from which many of the best practices have been established. Readers are encouraged to explore these references, particularly those that fall outside the scope of their primary discipline. Throughout, an underlying theme will become evident; the designer is ultimately responsible for the success or failure of the operator. Put bluntly, the operator's inability to comprehend or effectively use an interface is the fault of the designer. Errors incurred due to flawed design are not the responsibility of the operator, but are instead, evidence of a failure on the part of the designer. It is our expectation that readers of this guidance are willing to accept that responsibility and do their best to optimize their operator's experience.

[0005] Success in the above endeavor, however, is not possible if designers work in isolation. Designers must collaborate with operators to understand their goals and the environment in which they work. Additionally, designers must collaborate with their peers to examine their work with a critical eye and to better understand successful designs and the underlying mechanisms that help achieve the desired outcome. The recommendations provided here advocate these additional steps as they are essential to the development of optimally usable designs.

2.2 Goals

[0006] As stated previously, this guidance is targeted at designers for the benefit of operators. By improving the environment in which operators work, producers will ideally realize reductions in downtime, increased throughput, lower levels of rework, and operators with higher levels of job satisfaction. Moreover, standardization in specific areas of the Human-Machine interface should aid in the development of cross-functional packaging operators that can move from production line to production line with greater efficiency.

[0007] The best practices offered throughout are provided to encourage continous improvement on the part of system designers. Our ability to effectively communicate with our operators through the equipment we design can always be improved. The provided guidance is simply a starting point for designers seeking additional exposure to the usability and graphic design disciplines. Proper application of the provided recommendations is ultimately dependent on the context in question; evaluating the resulting designs via operator testing is essential. Getting it right the first time is a lofty goal, but getting it right is paramount. With a greater understanding and ability to control the way operator's experience the equipment we design, it is expected that – as designers – we will likewise experience greater job satisfaction.

3. Background

3.1 Operator Empathy

[0008] In order to appropriately design machines that will ultimately meet the needs of the operator, we must first establish the characteristics that are critical to success.

[0009] The first relevant operational characteristic, which distinguishes packaging equipment operators from most commercial product users, is the high likelihood that the operator is not present when feedback is provided by the machine. The operator responsible for multiple machines does not simply stand by and monitor a single HMI. When a downtime event is triggered by a specific machine in a complex line, it is highly likely that the operator is not present. Accordingly, effectively communicating with an operator that arrives after a critical event occurs will prove to be an essential skill.

[0010] The downtime example is further complicated by the urgency of the situation. If a machine is down, the producer is losing money. This additional urgency increases the cognitive load experienced by the operator. It is reasonable to argue that the likelihood of interface-manipulation errors will increase under these conditions. Poor design is more easily accommodated under normal conditions. The added stress of abnormal conditions amplifies the impact of flawed designs. Accordingly, the designer must take care to consider the conditions and environment under which their operators perform.

[0011] The associated goals and context in which operators work should be in the forefront of the designer's mind as each HMI decision is made. Understanding and empathizing with the operator is a great motivator for the engaged designer.

3.2 Usability Characteristics

[0012] With a handful of critical operator characteristics understood, it is next important to establish the scope of the term usability. Nielsen (1993) presents a set of usability characteristics to help clarify the concept; the usability of a machine is a function of its a) Learnability, b) Memorability, c) Efficiency, d) Fault Restistance and Tolerance, and d) the level of Satisfaction experienced by the operator.

[0013] In the context of the packaging operator, Learnability, obviously, is most relevant to the new line operator. Here, the operator's ability to quickly build fluency in the associated Human-Machine Interface is a key measure of the designer's success. Similarly, an operator's ability to become productive when transitioning from one machine to another is a function of the new machine's Learnability level. It is our expectation that the standardization initiatives established in this guideline will increase the Learnability of any machines adopting the standards.

[0014] Learnability is generally measured by presenting new users with a task to perform using the interface. The time required to complete the task is a reasonable quantitative metric. When compared to an alternate design, the magnitude of the improvement attributed to the new design will help validate the design.

[0015] In a well functioning production line, it is likely that an operator will have infrequent interactions with the HMI. When the operator returns to a machine after a prolonged absence, the Memorability of the HMI becomes the focal point. Will the operator need to relearn the interface or is there some level of retention that allows a returning user to return to productivity more quickly than a new user?

[0016] Maintaining the downtime example discussed earlier, an operator's ability to quickly identify and resolve an event is a perfect demonstration of the Efficiency characteristic. If instead, the operator must struggle through multiple steps with uncertainty and confusion, the downtime event will likely persist for an unacceptable period. When considered as raw metrics, the Efficiency of a packaging machine is best characterized in terms of the time necessary to complete specific tasks. While it is often argued that the number of steps taken (e.g., taps, key presses) is a worthwhile metric, we argue that a single step that takes 5 minutes due to operator uncertainty is much less efficient than 50 steps that require minimal thought and take 10 seconds. While a lowering the number of required steps is a worthy objective, time to completion is usually the more important goal in the packaging context. Measuring efficiency often warrants some amount of task repetition. During such testing, the first few iterations will actually constitute a measure of learnability. Once the task is learned, a more accurate measure of efficiency can be achieved.

[0017] A system that helps operators avoid errors will likely pay dividends in both the Efficiency and Satisfaction contexts. Extra, unecessary HMI steps performed due to uncertainty on the part of the operator are a prime example of waste when considered from the lean manufacturing viewpoint. Producing designs that minimize such waste achieve the objective of Fault Resistance. Similarly, a system that can recover quickly from other errors (e.g., undo to recover from accidentally deleting a product configuration) warrants points in the Fault Tolerance category. Again, this characteristic clearly impacts the Efficiency and Satisfaction measures.

[0018] While the Learnability, Memorability, Efficency, and Fault Awareness characteristics are well aligned with quantitative metrics, the Satisfaction characteristic is much more qualitative in nature. 3.3 Situational Awareness

[0019] Given the previously discussed usability characterisics, it is next necessary to consider how a given machine can effectively (a) communicate a condition and (b) direct the operator to the appropriate course of action. Endsley (1995) offers a model for Situational Awareness (SA) that breaks such interactions into levels. First, the model declares that the operator must perceive a given condition. Perception could be via a horn, a stack light, a message on a display, a pile of products on the production floor, or the smell of burning plastic. Having achieved Level 1 SA, the operator must then appropriately interpret the situation. What does the light mean? Why are there items on the floor? What's that smell? Finally, after Level 2 SA is achieved, the operator must project the current situation into the future. Perhaps the light is only a warning indicator that requires action to prevent a less desirable outcome. At this stage, the operator has achieved Level 3 SA and must make a decision to, optionally, perform some course of action. Ideally, this awareness is not flawed and contains no level of uncertainty. The appropriateness of the response is contingent on the effectiveness at each subsequent level.

[0020] The designer is resposible for ensuring that the situational awareness is not flawed and contains minimal uncertainty. If the operator does not perceive the light because it is obscured from their vantage point, they have no reason to consider its meaning. Or, perhaps, a horn from a different line distracted their attention. Ensuring that the stimulus is effectively percieved is the first challenge.

[0021] Comprehension of the situation with respect to the operator's goals is the next challenge. Given a fully integrated production line and a standard stack light convention, the operator will be responsible for monitoring the lights on the entire production line. If the operator observes a light transition from green to blue, the operator must interpret that light transition both in the context of the individual machine, and in relationship to the other machines on the line. Once both the observation and comprehension are archieved, the operator will determine, for example, that the wrapping machine is running low on film. Having made this determination, the operator will consider the level 3 needs of the situation, which consider how the current state will impact future status of the line.

[0022] Continuing the example of the machine running low on film, the operator will likely consider how urgent the situation is. If an additional 15 minutes of production is expected following the alarm, the operator will consider this point when making a decision on a course of action.

[0023] Understanding this basic model of situation awareness is a great benefit to designers. By understanding the basic thought process of the operator, and the relevant levels therein, the designer is left with only a few basic questions when evaluating a design. Will the operator see the stimulus I have designed? If the stimulus is provided on screen, is the intended focal point sufficiently salient, or is there other "digital noise" distracting the operator's attention from the critical indicator. Will the operator comprehend the feedback from the machine, or is the signal ambiguous, leaving the operator with a high level of uncertainty. Finally, does the machine simplify the operator's ability to predict the future outcome? Does the system warrant an explicit message communicating the time remaining until a critical system stoppage? When considering the appropriateness of the design, the designer can utilize the situation awareness model to help direct and evaluate potential solutions.

[0024] In the next section of the guidance, we will begin by presenting the PackML Stack Light standard. Throughout the explanation of that standard, we will consider the associated aspects of usability and the components of the situational awareness model that are relevant in each aspect of its design. Once these concepts are considered in the simple context of stack lights, we will continue to the more complicated user-interface design domain.

4. Stack Lights and Control Push-Buttons

4.1 Scope

[0025] The implementation guidance in the area of stack lights and push-button controls offers a unique opportunity to illustrate the basic usability principles that will be essential in the far more flexible areas of the HMI (e.g., user-interface displays). Whereas stack lights are limited to...

  • a selection of colors
  • in a particular order
  • illuminated at a particular frequency based on the machine state

the graphical display portion of the HMI affords the designer a more limitless range of options, which, unfortunately, offers endless opportunities for the introduction of usability issues. Fortunately, the same simple principles that are inherent in the effective design of stack lights and control push-buttons will demonstrate the foundation of usability in the graphical interface. Discussing these principles in the more tangible context of stack lights and push-buttons may at first seem excessive, but, as we will discover, the design principles that produce optimal usability are quite simple and intuitive once they are understood and embraced. When applied to the graphical portion of the HMI, these principles will improve system usability, with minimal effort on the part of the designer.

[0026] While the actual requirements necessary to specify the standardized PackML stack light and push-button implementations can be expressed in a few short paragraphs, we will offer a more verbose description that highlights the associated usability principles that are likely taken for granted.

4.2 Standard Implementation

[0027] The standard PackML light stack presents select colors representing the PackML states described in ANSI/ISA TR88.00.02-2015. Ordered from the top down as presented in Table 1, the lights will be illuminated in the color and sequence specified based on the current PackML state.

Table 1: PackML Stack Light Standard

Red Stopped Solid
Amber Suspended, Upstream Stoppage Flashing
Amber Suspended, Downstream Stoppage Solid

Blue Held Solid
Green Idle Flashing
Green Execute Solid

The standard implementation for push-button controls (e.g., start, stop, reset) follow conventions similar to those defined for stack lights. While adhering to the basic color and marking requirements for push-butttons specified in IEC 60204-1:2016, the PackML standard requires that illuminated push-button actuators use the sequence mapping specified in Table 2.

4.2 Hierarchy, Orientation, Consistency, and Convention

[0028] Given the associated PackML states, the top-down orientation of the lights follows western cultural conventions where the most critical elements are positioned at the top. Here, the Stopped and Aborted states are presented at the top of the stack light, communicating their importance through their color (e.g., red or amber) and, perhaps more importantly, their position. The sequence attribute (i.e., flashing or solid) further communicates the urgency of the current state. It is accepted that the flashing light will draw more attention than a solid light, therefore, the flashing sequence is associated with the more urgent PackML states (e.g., Aborted is more urgent than Stopped). Here the attributes of color, position, and sequence represent hierarchy within the state model. Lights in a higher position are more urgent than those in a lower position, while lights that are flashing are more urgent than those that are solid. Similarly, through cultural convention, red is more urgent than amber. Hierarchy in the graphical interface will utilize these same attributes, as well as others, to establish focus, guiding the operator's attention to elements that are most essential to the task at hand.

[0029] Given this summary, a key requirement of the proper application of the PackML stack light standard is an adherence to the specified light orientation. Across a given production line, it is essential that the order of the lights maintain the top-down orientation listed in Table 1. While the dual coding (i.e., color and position) within the standard would likely accommodate a mispositioned light (e.g., amber above red), such a violation would surely be a distraction to a line operator. It is expected that a focused color-sighted line operator could overcome a violation of the light position standard, however, under the additional stress of a line stoppage, a simple lack of consistency in light stack orientation could lead to an extended stoppage. Accordingly, we will highlight consistency in orientation as a key usability principle, one which will be revisited in the discussion on navigation design.

[0030] Moreover, it is essential that the color and sequence attributes of the associated stack lights be implemented in a consistent fashion within a single production facility. While an operator will most likely overcome some level of inconsistency in the size or color of the red stack lights across a number of machines in a single production line, a deviation in the frequency of a flashing light could draw undue attention to a single machine. A machine stack light flashing at a higher rate will draw more attention than surrounding machines blinking at a lower rate. Such scenarios should be avoided by establishing a standard flashing frequency within the line.

These points illustrate the relevant distinction between internal and external consistency. Utilizing the same size and shape lenses within a single stack light of a given machine demonstrates the most basic application of internal consistency. A lack of consistency in a single light of the stack – a different shape or size, for example – would draw additional attention from the operator. This should be avoided unless the additional attention is a desirable effect. Conversely, using the same top-down sequence of colors in the PackML stack lights across a production line is a more critical example of external consistency. While not discussed in IEC 60204-1:2009, external consistency in the relative orientation of the push-button controls for Start, Stop, and Reset across machines is highly recommended. As with the light stack example – where color and position combine to communicate state – the dual coding of marking and positioning of push-buttons reduces the likelihood of error on the part of the operator. However, consider the impact of two adjacent machines where the orientation of the Start and Stop buttons are reveresed. While inconsistency in orientation should be avoided if possible, the optional introduction of color can aid the operator when positional consistency cannot be guaranteed. However, but any inconsistency in this orientation should be avoided if possible. Depending on the context, internal consistency can prove more critical than external consistency. Depending on the context, internal consistency can be more critical than external consistency.

4.3 Proximity

[0031] Given the importance of the orientation principle, it is equally important that the PackML stack lights and push-button controls are positioned in a manner that effectively communicate their association with a given machine. While operator visibility is a key requirement for effective positioning, a stack light that is not sufficiently positioned with respect to the machine could fail to effectively communicate the PackML state. Moreover, a PackML stack light that is not consistently positioned within the relative body of the associated machine may not be appropriately identified if multiple stack lights with similar colors are present within the machine's proximity. Accordingly, it is recommended that the PackML stack lights are positioned such that a line operator with a view of the production line can consistently identify the PackML stack lights for each machine and correctly interpret the machine responsible for a line stoppage or held event.

4.4 Contrast

[0032] While it is again expected that sufficient contrast will exist between a lit and unlit stack light, we will belabor the example to highlight the related design concept. Within a stack light, it is expected that an individual lamp will provide sufficient brightness to ensure that an operator can effectively distinguish between the lit and unlit states. Likewise, it is expected that the contrast between lamps (e.g., red to amber) is sufficient to ensure that the operator will correctly, without hesitation, identify the actively lit lamp. It is important to note here that the operator's ability to interpret the correct lamp states is a function of both the the lights themselves (e.g., brightness, color) and the surrounding environment. Specifically it is critical to consider the operator's perspective when viewing the lamps. It is possible that the structures behind a stack light will impair the operator's ability to correctly perceive the active lamp.

4.5 Speaking the Operator's Language

[0033] While it is reasonable to use the conventions of position, color, and sequence to draw an operator's attention to a given machine, it is still necessary to communicate the required course of action in a manner that speaks more directly and effectively to the operator. It is not sufficient to blink the blue light and expect the operator to know what action is necessary. The graphical interface must effectively communicate the meaning of the blinking blue light by presenting an instruction that leads to a prompt resolution.

[0034] Operators that have been drawn to a machine with an effective stack light indication should be greeted with a message they can appropriately identify and act upon. Hierarchy within the graphical interface is essential for this task. The approaching operator should quickly identify the required action when they scan the graphical interface. Instructions that lack prominence will delay the recovery of the production line. Lack of contrast and emphasis (e.g., size, boldness, color) will reduce the saliency of the operator instruction. Be sure the instruction presented to the operator is easily identified. Test the implementation to ensure the instructions are identified in a consistent manner.

[0035] Avoiding generic instructions (e.g., HELD) in favor of machine specific instructions (e.g., Empty the Under Bin and Press Start) will expedite operator response and line recovery. Where appropriate, utilize conventions like Simplified English, which communicate to a broader audience through grammatical conventions. Avoid abbreviations, jargon, acronyms, and engineering-centric language, which will likely be lost on line operators. Reserve engineering conventions for log files, which will not require interpretation from line operators.
